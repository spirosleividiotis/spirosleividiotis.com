Case 01

Multi-Step Progressive Bar

A progress indicator for multi-step flows. The challenge: each step needs to feel connected, not discrete. The bar grows continuously, with steps overlapping by 250ms—creating a seamless, uninterrupted progression. Custom cubic-beziers at three keyframes give it personality: quick start, smooth mid-transition, confident finish.

Interactive Preview
UX Challenge
Why overlap steps? Users perceive discrete jumps as "loading breaks." By starting step 2 before step 1 finishes (250ms overlap), the progression feels continuous—like the system is always working, never waiting. The custom easing at mid-point (600ms) adds a subtle "breath" that makes the motion feel alive, not robotic.

Technical Specs

Property Specification
Duration motion-duration-1500 1500ms
Delay motion-delay-0 for Step 1
Step 2: 1250ms (starts 250ms before Step 1 ends)
Step 3: 2500ms (same overlap pattern)
Easing Custom cubic-bezier keyframes:
At start [0ms]: 0.67, -0.02, 0.8, 0.15
At mid [600ms]: 0.03, 0.07, 0, 1
At end [1500ms]: 0, 0.8, 0.2, 1
Scale Custom scale keyframes:
At start [0ms]: 0%
At mid [600ms]: 75%
At end [1500ms]: 100%
Multi-Step Rule
Each subsequent step keeps the same duration but starts 250ms before the previous step ends by reducing its delay accordingly. Example: Step 1 ends at 1500ms → Step 2 delay = 1250ms. This creates continuous overlapping progression instead of discrete jumps.
Case 02

Tertiary Button Arrow Interaction

A text link with an animated arrow. The challenge: make the interaction feel fluid and intentional—on hover, the arrow slides out and back in within a mask; on click, it scales down and up for tactile feedback. This micro-interaction reinforces that it's clickable while maintaining consistency with button behaviors across the site.

Interactive Preview
UX Challenge
Why dual arrows? The arrow appears to move, but it's actually two arrows in sequence—one exits while another enters. This creates the illusion of continuous motion within a rectangular mask. The 150ms stagger prevents them from overlapping visually. On click, the scale down/up (100% → 90% → 100%) provides haptic-like feedback—confirming the action without requiring a physical press.

Technical Specs

Interaction Specification
Click Scale Duration: motion-duration-300 300ms
Delay: motion-delay-0
Easing: motion-easing-smooth 0.4, 0, 0.6, 1
Scale: 100% → 90% (15 frames) → 100% (30 frames)

(First Arrow)		 Duration: motion-duration-300 300ms
Delay: motion-delay-0
Easing: motion-easing-smooth 0.4, 0, 0.6, 1
Translate X: 0px → 18px
Translate Y: 0px → -18px
Hover In
(Second Arrow)		 Duration: motion-duration-300 300ms
Delay: motion-delay-150 150ms

Translate X: 18px → 0px
Translate Y: -18px → 0px
Implementation Note
The hover and click effects trigger when hovering either the text or the arrow. On mobile, only the click effect applies (no hover state).
Case 03

Card Hover Lift Interaction

A hover effect for interactive cards across the website. The challenge: make cards feel responsive and tangible without being heavy. On hover, the card lifts 10px upward—subtle enough to feel light, but pronounced enough to signal interactivity. The effect only applies to clickable cards, preserving the visual hierarchy and preventing meaningless motion.

Interactive Previews (3 Variations)
UX Challenge
Why 10px lift? The 10px translateY creates a subtle elevation change—enough to feel like the card is "rising" toward you, but not so much that it feels jumpy or aggressive. The same timing in/out (250ms) maintains symmetry—hover on feels as smooth as hover off. Critically, this only applies to clickable cards. Non-interactive cards don't lift, preventing false affordances.

Technical Specs

Interaction Specification
Hover On Duration: motion-duration-250 250ms
Delay: motion-delay-0
Easing: motion-easing-default 0.4, 0, 0.2, 1
Translate Y: 0px → -10px
Hover Off Duration: motion-duration-250 250ms
Delay: motion-delay-0
Easing: motion-easing-default 0.4, 0, 0.2, 1
Translate Y: -10px → 0px
Implementation Note
This hover applies to all clickable cards: Bento, Cards (all variations), Cards within Hero. Slider cards do not use these specs. Arrow animation specs are handled separately.
Case 04

Card Flip Interaction

A physical card customization flow. The challenge: make the flip feel tangible—like you're flipping a real card, not swapping JPEGs. 3D rotation with proper perspective, staggered layer reveals on the back side, and attention to the "weight" of the animation. It's not just eye candy—it helps users understand they're looking at a different side of the same object.

Animation Preview
UX Challenge
Why 3D rotation? A 2D cross-fade would be faster to implement, but it wouldn't convey the spatial relationship between front and back. The 3D flip creates a mental model: "I'm looking at the back of the same card." This helps users understand the customization context—they're not navigating to a new screen, they're exploring the object. The animation reinforces the product hierarchy.
